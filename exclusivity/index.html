The Hall of Fame and the Myth of Exclusivity

“The Hall of Fame is for the immortals of the game—Ted Williams, Babe Ruth, Walter Johnson…”

You hear people say things like this when discussing the Hall of Fame credentials for players like Tim Raines, Alan Trammell, or Larry Walker. Everyone agrees they were good players. But some people simply believe that the Hall of Fame should be reserved for players of a much higher caliber.

The only thing is… it’s not. And it hasn't been since the 1930s.

Thanks to the Hall of wWAR, we now have a list of which Hall of Famers actually deserve to be there. Currently, 63 of the 208 players in the Hall of Fame (30.3%) didn’t deserve to be inducted (and deserve to be replaced by better players). For full analysis, see the post at Beyond the Box Score.

Cumulative Percentage of Deserving Hall of Famers

In this table shows the percentage of deserving Hall of Famers changing over time. Some interesting points:

  • The Veterans selections from 1945 and 1946 brought the percentage from 100% down to 81.1%.
  • From 1970 to 1975, a particularly poor stretch brought the percentage from 74.0% to 68.6%.
  • 1987’s induction of Catfish Hunter represented the all-time low point (66.9%).
  • The 1980s didn’t do much to help. After 1989, the percentage still sat at 68.2%.
  • It has been slowly rebounding since then, with 39 of the last 50 inductees being worthy choices (78.0%).
